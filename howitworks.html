How does menstruation work?

Menstruation is part of the menstrual cycle, which typically lasts about 28 days but can range from 21 to 35 days. The cycle has several phases:

  1. Menstrual Phase (Days 1-5):

    During this phase, the uterine lining (endometrium) is shed and expelled through the vagina, resulting in bleeding. This bleeding is a mixture of blood, mucus, and endometrial tissue.

  2. Follicular Phase (Days 1-13):

    This phase begins on the first day of menstruation and continues until ovulation. The pituitary gland releases follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), which stimulates the ovaries to produce follicles. Each follicle contains an egg, and as the follicles develop, the endometrium starts to thicken in preparation for a potential pregnancy.

  3. Ovulation (Day 14):

    Triggered by a surge in luteinizing hormone (LH), one mature egg is released from a dominant follicle in the ovary. The egg travels down the fallopian tube, where it may be fertilized by sperm.

  4. Luteal Phase (Days 15-28):

    After ovulation, the ruptured follicle forms a structure called the corpus luteum, which secretes progesterone to maintain the thickened endometrium. If the egg is not fertilized, the corpus luteum breaks down, leading to a drop in progesterone and estrogen levels. This hormonal decrease triggers the start of a new menstrual cycle as the endometrium is shed.
